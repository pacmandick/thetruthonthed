🎭 The Scene I Didn't Audition For

By Emmanuel Tarango
TruthOnTheD.com

Shortly after I moved to Dallas, I met a man named Tim Johnson. He worked in theater — a managing director, well-spoken, professional. The kind of person you'd assume would know the difference between right and wrong, and how to treat people with basic dignity. We hung out a few times. He seemed like good people.

Then one day, Tim shows up with this guy — Chris with the Mini. Yeah, I call him that on purpose. It’s a reference to his car, sure — but let’s be honest, it also works as a dig. Petty? Maybe. But that’s what he gets when you show up to someone’s place, act entitled, mock them, gaslight them, then deny it ever happened. He earned that nickname.

That day marked one of the most surreal, humiliating experiences I’ve ever had: both of them mocked me — openly, and then had the nerve to deny it.

I was in disbelief. I thought, no, maybe I’m reading into it too much. I tried to give them the benefit of the doubt. I tried to make sense of it. Surely someone like Tim — educated, professional, part of the creative world — wouldn’t go along with something like that. But he did. And it wasn’t subtle.

This was also the first time I met Chris. It wouldn’t be the last. I encountered him three more times. What’s strange is, I never remembered him — at all. I had such a mechanical way of removing toxic energy from my life, I literally forgot who he was each time. But each time, the vibe was the same: covert disrespect masked as sarcasm, the kind of fake laughs and backhanded comments that leave you questioning your own sanity.

The last time, I finally said something. I told him he was insecure, maybe even jealous. He had been a guest in my home — twice — and still felt entitled to disrespect me. Looking back, I see now what I was too naïve to accept: it was racism.

Chris is from South Africa — a country that clung to institutional racism under Apartheid well into the 1990s. Tim, meanwhile, is part of Dallas’s creative theater scene — an industry notorious for marginalizing and tokenizing people of color. Their behavior wasn’t an isolated case of rudeness. It was a pattern. One that said, you’re not welcome here.

What stunned me most was the realization that it didn’t matter who I was. Not my behavior. Not my education. Not my professionalism. None of it shielded me from being targeted. In fact, my confidence — my refusal to shrink myself — may have been what provoked it.

I didn’t want to believe that who I was — Mexican-American, masculine, spiritual, independent — was the problem. But now I know: it wasn’t me. It was them. And it was systemic.

I debated how to tell this story. I chose to use names — not out of revenge, but for accountability. Because “Tim and Chris” aren’t just metaphors. They’re real people. They helped set the tone for what would become an ongoing pattern of harassment and psychological abuse in Dallas.

Even after I left the city, it followed me.

  • My iCloud was hacked.
  • My emails, medical records, financial files, and personal photos were stolen.
  • Compromising videos were taken without my knowledge and, I believe, distributed.
  • People would approach me and reference private facts about my life — things no one should know.

This group not only had access to my digital life — they used it to target me, sabotage my job, disrupt my housing, and attack my mental health. Even my landlords and employers were reached. Emails were sent in my name that I didn’t write. Conversations I never had were orchestrated without my knowledge.

In Texas, “revenge porn” is supposed to be automatically referred to the District Attorney. But when I asked Detective Joshua Brooks of the Dallas Police Department to refer my case, I was stonewalled. He told me he didn’t handle that kind of crime and redirected me elsewhere. I followed up. I got silence.
Meanwhile, the Dallas County District Attorney, Michael O’Brien, is close personal friends with people in the group — including Scott Jerold McCartney, the man I believe orchestrated much of this abuse.

I asked for justice. What I got was indifference, protectionism, and institutional silence.

This blog is my voice. My truth. My record.

I don’t have the power to issue subpoenas. I can’t force Apple, Meta, or Google to reveal who logged into my accounts. I can’t access the IMEI numbers of the dozens of people who impersonated me on dating apps. But I can tell my story.

And by doing so, I will:

  • Hold people accountable
  • Give voice to others who’ve been targeted and silenced
  • Prove that no one is alone in this fight
  • And make it undeniable: we will get justice — with or without permission

This isn’t revenge. This is exposure.
